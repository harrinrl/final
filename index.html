Laws restricting women's access to reproductive health care increase unintended pregnancies

States with laws limiting abortion or contraception access is linked to higher unintended pregnancy rates

By Rebecca Harrington

More than half of pregnancies across the U.S. every year are unintended. This means they were either mistimed (31 percent) or unwanted (20 percent) and resulted in a birth (60 percent) or an abortion (40 percent). Each year, this results in 3.4 million pregnancies that were unintentional. This number is shockingly high. So what’s driving unintended pregnancy rates?

Kelli Garcia, senior counsel for the National Women’s Law Center, said the biggest factors increasing the number of unintended pregnancies are contraception education and access. Abstinence-only education has been shown to drive unintended pregnancy rates up, Garcia said. During the month of conception that resulted in an unintended pregnancy, 54 percent of women said they did not use birth control at all, and 41 percent said they had used it inconsistently. This data, and the rates on unintended pregnancy, is according to the Guttmacher Institute, an organization that tracks and supports reproductive health rights. “Everyone needs to be taught how to protect themselves in order to prevent unintended pregnancies,” Garcia said.

Since the 1980s, unintended pregnancies have become concentrated among low-income women, according to the Guttmacher Institute, making government assistance a key driver. Public funding of contraception has been shown to have an effect on pregnancy rates, Garcia said, since cost can be a significant barrier to obtaining birth control. The Affordable Care Act improved access to free birth control since it requires insurance programs cover contraception. Garcia said the full effects of the ACA haven’t trickled down yet, so it may take a few years before unintended pregnancy rates decrease because of the increased access to contraception.

The good news is that the rate decreased for a majority of states from 2002 to 2010. This was likely because of increased awareness, access and use of birth control, particularly long-term options like IUDs, according to Guttmacher Institute principal research scientist Kathryn Kost. But these numbers remain stagnant in the south, where polices on contraception tend to be more restrictive.

Texas, for example, passed legislation restricting access to abortion clinics and to family planning services, which include contraception, consultation and health screening like pap smears. The Affordable Care Act made it possible for women to receive family planning services paid for by Medicaid if they make 250 percent of the poverty level, a distinction that covers many more women than Medicaid usually can. In 2011, the Texas legislature decided it would no longer accept federal money to follow the national program, and instead fund family planning services on its own so it could exclude Planned Parenthood and restrict access to abortions and contraception. Elizabeth Nash, the senior state issues associate for the Guttmacher Institute, said hard numbers aren’t in yet to show unintended pregnancy rates increasing in Texas because of this change, but the state health department found 77 percent fewer women were provided family planning services in 2013 than in 2011.

For women who find themselves pregnant when they don’t want to be, access to abortions and emergency contraception can be imperative to their health and finances, and the health and economics of society as a whole. The Guttmacher Institute estimated that unintended pregnancies cost the government alone $21 billion in 2010. So how do these laws stack up state-by-state? When health services and education aren’t available, Nash said, a whole constellation of negative effects can abound — including not recognizing pregnancy early enough to have an abortion, becoming trapped in unhealthy relationships, and being unfamiliar with the symptoms of STIs. Ideally, she said states should provide medically sound sexual education, adequately fund family planning services, locate clinics where they can be easily accessed, inform women these services are confidential, and ensure the full range of reproductive health care is available and affordable. “Deciding when to become a parent is a very serious decision — one that is just fundamental to one’s autonomy,” Nash said.

Garcia said the National Women’s Law Center would like to see all of the policies here change to “meets.” Required waiting periods and biased counseling periods in particular, she said, are unnecessary burdens to getting abortions that shame women and make it harder to take care of their health. “Policies that restrict access to health care,” she said, “send a message that politicians don’t trust women to make their own decisions about their health care.”
